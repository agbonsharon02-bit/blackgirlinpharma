Pharmacovigilance

01

What Is Pharmacovigilance?

Pharmacovigilance (PV), also called Drug Safety, is the science and activities related to detecting, assessing, understanding, and preventing adverse effects of medicines. While clinical trials test drugs in controlled settings, PV monitors what happens in the real world — across millions of patients with diverse conditions, medications, and risk factors.

PV professionals process Individual Case Safety Reports (ICSRs), detect emerging safety signals, write Periodic Safety Update Reports (PSUR/PBRER), and work with regulators worldwide to manage risk. Every approved drug has an ongoing PV program behind it.

In Canada, PV is governed by Health Canada under the Food and Drugs Act and its regulations, including Section C.01.016, which mandates reporting of serious unexpected adverse drug reactions (SUADRs). Health Canada's Canada Vigilance Program and the MedEffect Canada portal serve as the primary channels for adverse reaction reporting. Canada follows ICH E2 guidelines for international safety reporting standards, ensuring alignment with global pharmacovigilance practices.

Quick Fact

The WHO's VigiBase — the world's largest pharmacovigilance database — now contains over 40 million case reports, and that number grows every single day. Behind every one of those reports is a PV professional ensuring the data is captured, assessed, and acted upon.

Without Pharmacovigilance, the risks of approved medicines would go undetected and unmanaged after they reach patients. That is how essential this function is to public health and the integrity of the pharmaceutical industry.

02

Why Choose Pharmacovigilance?

If you are passionate about patient safety, detail-oriented by nature, and want a pharma career with real flexibility and global relevance, Pharmacovigilance is an outstanding choice. Here are four compelling reasons why PV professionals love what they do:

Patient Safety Mission

You are the last line of defense protecting patients from unknown drug risks. Every signal you detect and every report you process has the potential to save lives.

Growing Demand

Global PV regulations are expanding — every company with a marketed product needs PV expertise. Demand for qualified professionals continues to outpace supply.

Remote-Friendly

PV is one of the most work-from-home-compatible careers in pharma. Much of the work — case processing, report writing, signal review — can be done entirely remotely.

Transferable Skills

PV expertise translates across therapeutic areas, company types, and regulatory regions. Your skills are valued at sponsors, CROs, biotech startups, and consulting firms alike.

03

Skills You'll Need

A strong PV professional combines scientific literacy with meticulous attention to detail and an understanding of global regulatory requirements. You do not need a clinical background to excel — but you do need to be thorough, systematic, and deeply committed to accuracy.

Adverse Event / ICSR Processing

Collecting, evaluating, and submitting Individual Case Safety Reports within regulatory timelines — the foundational skill of every PV role.

MedDRA Coding

Applying the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities to accurately classify adverse events using standardized terminology across global databases.

Signal Detection & Assessment

Identifying patterns in safety data that may indicate a new or changing risk — and evaluating whether those signals warrant regulatory action.

PSUR / PBRER Writing

Authoring Periodic Safety Update Reports (PSURs) and Periodic Benefit-Risk Evaluation Reports (PBRERs) — the ICH E2C(R2) format used by Health Canada and regulators globally — for submission to regulatory authorities.

Risk Management Plans (RMP / Risk Management Plan (Health Canada))

Developing and maintaining risk minimization strategies required by Health Canada and other regulators to manage known and potential risks of approved products.

Regulatory Submissions & Agency Communication

Submitting safety data to Health Canada, the EMA, and other health authorities, and managing responses to agency queries and inspections.

Bonus Skill

Proficiency in safety databases like Argus Safety, ARISg, or Oracle Empirica Signal Detection is highly valued. Many companies will train you on their specific platform, but familiarity with at least one puts you ahead.

05

Career Pathway

Pharmacovigilance offers a well-defined and rewarding career ladder. Most professionals enter at the associate level processing ICSRs, then build expertise in signal detection, aggregate reporting, and risk management as they advance into senior and leadership positions.

1

Entry Level · 0–2 Years

PV Associate / Drug Safety Associate

Process incoming adverse event reports, perform MedDRA coding, conduct literature searches, and support ICSR submission activities under the guidance of senior PV staff.

$55K–$75K CAD BS/MS degree GCP basics ICSR processing MedDRA coding
2

Mid-Level · 2–5 Years

PV Specialist / Safety Scientist

Lead signal detection activities, author PSUR sections, manage agency communications for assigned products, and mentor junior associates on case processing quality.

$75K–$102K CAD Signal detection PSUR/PBRER writing Agency communication
3

Senior Level · 5–10 Years

Senior PV Specialist / PV Manager

Oversee Risk Management Plans, manage a team of PV professionals, lead global regulatory submissions, and serve as the primary safety contact for assigned product programs.

$102K–$135K CAD RMP oversight Team management Global submissions
4

Leadership · 10+ Years

Director / VP Global Drug Safety

Own the full PV strategy for an entire portfolio. Represent your company at Health Canada's Office of Pharmacovigilance and Epidemiology and EMA meetings, interface with executive leadership on safety matters, and shape global drug safety policy.

$135K–$205K+ CAD Portfolio safety strategy Executive interface Health Canada / EMA interactions

Pro Tip

CROs and PV service organizations like IQVIA, Clario, and Worldwide Clinical Trials process thousands of ICSRs weekly. Starting here gives you rapid, high-volume case experience — the fastest way to build the technical fluency that sponsors look for when hiring in-house.

06

Interview Tips

PV interviews test both your technical knowledge and your ability to demonstrate the precision this field demands. Here are the most important things to prepare before walking into any drug safety interview:

01

Know the Regulatory Timelines

In Canada, the mandatory timeline is 15-day reporting for serious unexpected adverse drug reactions (SUADRs) under Section C.01.016 of Health Canada's Food and Drug Regulations. Be able to explain what qualifies as a SUADR, when the clock starts, and how Canadian timelines align with ICH E2A standards.

02

Understand the Core Definitions

Know the difference between an adverse event, adverse drug reaction, and serious adverse event — these distinctions matter in every interview. Mixing them up signals a fundamental gap.

03

Prepare a Case Narrative Example

Be ready to describe how you would write an ICSR narrative clearly and completely — including what elements are required, how you would handle missing information, and how you would ensure accuracy.

04

Demonstrate Attention to Detail

PV errors can reach regulators and affect product labels — show that you understand the stakes. Come to the interview with a flawless resume and speak with precision about processes and timelines.

05

Research the Company's Marketed Portfolio

Know their key products, any recent label updates, and whether they have a Risk Management Plan (Health Canada) for any of their medicines. This level of preparation consistently impresses hiring managers.

06

Ask About Their Safety Database and Signal Detection Methodology

Asking which safety database they use and how their signal detection process is structured shows you understand the operational side of the role — and it signals you are already thinking like a PV professional.

Growth Outlook

Global pharmacovigilance regulations are tightening across Health Canada, the EMA, and emerging markets simultaneously, driving sustained demand for PV professionals in Canada and internationally. The WHO's VigiBase now contains over 40 million case reports — and that number grows daily. Companies with complex biologics, gene therapies, and combination products face the most demanding PV requirements, creating opportunities for Canadian specialists who can navigate both domestic Health Canada obligations and global reporting standards.

08

Resources to Get Started

You do not have to figure this out alone. These are the most valuable organizations, databases, and platforms for anyone building a career in Pharmacovigilance:

DIA (diaglobal.org)

Drug Information Association — PV training, conferences, and a global professional community for drug safety practitioners.

EudraVigilance (ema.europa.eu)

The EMA's safety database — review it to understand European pharmacovigilance requirements and real-world case data in practice.

MedWatch / FDA (fda.gov/safety)

The US adverse event reporting portal and source for FDA drug safety communications, label updates, and REMS information.

WHO VigiBase (who.int)

The world's largest pharmacovigilance database. Understanding VigiBase and how signal detection works within it signals real expertise to employers.

SCOPE PV Training (scopepv.org)

Free and low-cost training modules covering PV fundamentals — an ideal starting point for anyone new to the field.
