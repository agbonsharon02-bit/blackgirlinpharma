Regulatory Affairs vs Quality Assurance — Which Is Right for You?

Two of the most popular non-lab careers in pharma. Both respected, both well-paid, both deeply technical — but they suit very different kinds of people. Here's how to tell which one is yours.

What Each Career Actually Is

Regulatory Affairs and Quality Assurance are two of the most popular non-lab career paths in pharma — and they're frequently confused, sometimes lumped together, and occasionally applied for interchangeably. The overlap makes sense on paper: both roles care deeply about regulations, both require sharp attention to detail, and both touch almost every other function in the company. But once you're in either role, the day-to-day experience is quite different.

Regulatory Affairs (RA)

RA is about getting medicines approved and keeping them compliant once they're on the market. Regulatory professionals build and manage the relationship between a pharmaceutical company and government health agencies — in Canada, that's Health Canada; globally, it's bodies like the FDA, EMA, and others. They prepare and submit the scientific documentation packages that regulators use to decide whether a drug is safe and effective enough to approve. They also monitor ongoing regulatory requirements, manage post-market changes, and advise the broader business on what's possible within the regulatory landscape.

Without RA, no medicine reaches a pharmacy shelf. That is not an exaggeration.

Quality Assurance (QA)

QA is about making sure everything in the development, manufacturing, and testing of a medicine meets the required standards — and that when something goes wrong, the right people know about it and the process gets fixed. QA professionals own the quality management system: they write and manage SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures), investigate deviations and batch failures, conduct internal audits, and lead the response when a health agency walks through the door for an inspection. They are the internal enforcement function — responsible for the integrity of every process behind the product.

One way to think about it: RA talks to the government. QA polices the process. Both roles are essential. Both carry real organisational authority. And both can lead to director and VP-level careers.

What a Typical Day Looks Like

A day in RA

You might spend the morning reviewing a clinical summary before it goes into a Health Canada submission, tracking down a missing Chemistry, Manufacturing and Controls (CMC) data package from the formulations team, and preparing agenda items for an upcoming pre-submission meeting. After lunch, there's a cross-functional alignment call about a proposed label change, followed by a conversation with legal about risk communication requirements. You end the day writing a strategic memo summarising where a product sits against current Health Canada guidance. You have toggled between scientific data, regulatory strategy, writing, and cross-functional coordination — sometimes all before noon.

A day in QA

Your morning starts with reviewing an overnight batch record investigation from the manufacturing floor. You chair a CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) review at 10am, holding the team accountable to the timeline. After lunch, you review a proposed SOP revision, give feedback to a QA analyst, and audit a documentation package before it goes to the archive. You spend the last hour of the day preparing evidence for an upcoming Health Canada inspection. You have used your authority to make calls, enforce standards, and close gaps — and the work you did today has a direct line to patient safety.

The Skills Each Career Rewards

RA rewards...

  • Strong scientific and regulatory writing
  • Strategic thinking across a product lifecycle
  • Regulatory intelligence — tracking evolving agency guidance
  • Cross-functional persuasion and negotiation
  • Comfort interpreting ambiguous regulatory guidance
  • Project management across multiple submission timelines

QA rewards...

  • Rigorous process thinking and systems design
  • Meticulous documentation and record-keeping
  • Investigation and root-cause analysis skills
  • Deep knowledge of GMP, GLP, GCP standards
  • Calm, authoritative presence under inspection pressure
  • Coaching and challenging people across functions

Pro Tip

Not sure which suits your brain? Think about how you solved your last big problem. Did you research the rules, write something structured, and convince someone? That's RA thinking. Did you identify the gap in a process, document it precisely, and build a fix that prevents it from happening again? That's QA thinking. Both are valuable — they're just wired differently.

Salary at Every Level

Both careers offer strong, growing compensation. RA carries a slight premium at mid-to-senior levels — partly because strategic regulatory expertise is genuinely scarce, and partly because experienced RA professionals often have significant influence over whether and when a product reaches market. That said, QA salaries are solidly competitive, and the VP of Quality at a major pharma company is one of the most powerful roles in the building.

Regulatory Affairs (CAD, base salary): Entry $58K–$78K · Specialist $78K–$105K · Senior/Manager $105K–$140K · Director $140K–$175K · VP $175K–$220K+

Quality Assurance (CAD, base salary): Entry $50K–$68K · Specialist $68K–$92K · Manager $92K–$125K · Director $125K–$158K · VP $158K–$195K+

Both paths also offer strong consulting rates ($90–$200/hr CAD) once you build deep expertise. Neither is a wrong financial choice.

Choose RA If You Love Writing and Strategy

Regulatory Affairs might be your path if any of these land:

  • You love reading and writing — long, complex documents — and you do it well
  • You enjoy interpreting grey areas: applying a general rule to a specific, novel situation
  • You're comfortable in strategic conversations with senior stakeholders and external agencies
  • You want to be involved across the whole drug lifecycle, from early clinical phases through to post-market commitments
  • You'd rather write a compelling regulatory justification than run an inspection audit

RA is a career for people who enjoy thinking big picture while caring deeply about the precision of the documents that make that picture a reality. If writing is genuinely one of your strengths and regulatory puzzles feel more like invitations than obstacles, this is your field.

Choose QA If You Love Systems and Standards

Quality Assurance might be your home if any of these sound familiar:

  • You are energised by processes — designing them, tightening them, enforcing them
  • You like finding the gap in a system and building the fix that closes it permanently
  • You're comfortable being the person who flags when something isn't good enough — and you have the presence to make that land
  • You want to be the person a company calls when Health Canada walks through the door
  • You'd rather write a tight SOP than a strategic submission narrative

QA professionals are some of the most respected people in any pharma organisation — because when they do their job well, everything else runs safely. The work is demanding, high-stakes, and deeply meaningful. If you have a systems brain and a high tolerance for detail and pressure, QA will reward you.

Can You Switch Between Them — and How?

Yes — more easily than most people assume, especially in the first five to seven years of your career. Both RA and QA are built on a foundation of GxP standards, regulatory frameworks, and documentation discipline. That shared foundation creates real mobility between the two.

The most travelled route is QA to RA. A solid QA background — deep GMP knowledge, inspection experience, quality system ownership — translates well into regulatory compliance and CMC (Chemistry, Manufacturing and Controls) regulatory roles, which sit at the intersection of the two functions. This is a recognised and respected move.

The reverse — RA to QA — happens too, usually through regulatory compliance, quality management, or roles at companies where RA and Quality are closely integrated (common in smaller biotechs and CROs).

If you're genuinely undecided, QA can be an excellent first move. It gives you deep exposure to GxP standards and regulatory enforcement that will serve you whether you stay in QA long-term or eventually pivot into RA strategy.

5 Questions to Help You Decide

If you're still sitting on the fence, work through these honestly:

  1. Do you prefer writing documents that persuade external audiences (regulatory submissions) — or internal ones (SOPs, investigation reports, audit findings)?
  2. Are you more energised by strategic planning and navigating regulatory ambiguity — or by building, enforcing, and improving operational systems?
  3. Does the idea of preparing for an agency pre-submission meeting (RA) or leading an inspection-readiness programme (QA) feel more natural?
  4. Would you rather work across the full drug lifecycle from early Phase I development — or focus on the quality processes that keep a product safely on the market?
  5. Which discomfort can you manage better: the ambiguity of a regulatory strategy decision, or the accountability of a quality system failure?

If your answers consistently pointed in one direction — that's your answer. If they split evenly, either path will grow you. Choose the one where the day-to-day work sounds more interesting, not just the end career it leads to.
