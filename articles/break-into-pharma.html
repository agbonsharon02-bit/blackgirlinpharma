How to Break Into Pharma With No Experience

You don't need to have worked in pharma to get your first pharma job. You just need to know how to position what you already have — and where to start looking.

6 min read · May 2026
  1. Home
  2. ›

The Myth That You Need Experience to Get Experience

Here's the thing nobody tells you when you're staring at a pharma job listing that asks for "2–3 years of industry experience": the pharmaceutical industry is full of people who started somewhere else. Your biology degree, your year working as a clinical coordinator at a hospital, your background managing projects — it all counts. What holds most people back isn't a lack of experience. It's not knowing how to name the experience they already have.

The so-called experience paradox — where you need experience to get experience — is real in some industries. Pharma is not one of them. Entry-level roles exist precisely because every pharma company, CRO (Contract Research Organisation), and biotech needs a steady pipeline of trained talent. Many of them are actively trying to diversify their workforce. That's a real opening. You should walk through it.

The first thing to do is stop filtering yourself out before you even apply. If you meet 60–70% of the requirements on a job description, apply. The worst they can say is no — and you'll have practised your pitch for next time.

What Entry-Level Employers Actually Look For

Before you tailor a single CV or hit apply on a single role, it helps to understand what hiring managers are actually evaluating at the entry level. Spoiler: it is not years of pharma-specific experience.

What they are genuinely looking for:

  • Attention to detail — pharma is a heavily regulated industry where documentation errors have real consequences. Show that you notice things other people miss.
  • Ability to follow and create processes — SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) run the industry. Employers want people who take procedures seriously and can learn them fast.
  • Clear written and verbal communication — whether you're writing regulatory submissions or presenting trial data, clarity matters.
  • Scientific or health literacy — for clinical and regulatory roles especially, a basic understanding of drug development or biological sciences gives you an edge.
  • Professionalism and reliability — this matters more than most people realise, particularly in field-based roles like clinical research where you're representing a company at external sites.

Notice what's not on that list: pharma-specific experience. Hiring managers at the entry level know you don't have it yet. They are hiring for potential. Your job is to prove you have it.

Quick Tip

Before you apply for any role, read the job description carefully and write down every single skill or quality they mention — then check how many you already have in a different context. Most candidates have more transferable skills than they realise. They just haven't named them yet.

The 5 Best Entry Points Into Pharma

Not every role is equally accessible when you're new to the industry. Some doors are genuinely easier to walk through. Here are the five that consistently offer the best chance of a first foothold:

1. Clinical Research Associate (CRA)

If you have a life sciences degree and are comfortable with travel, this is one of the best-paying entry-level roles in pharma. Many CROs run junior CRA or CRA trainee programmes specifically for recent graduates. You'll monitor clinical trials at hospital and clinic sites, ensuring the study runs according to protocol. It's demanding, but the career trajectory is fast — and the experience opens doors across the industry.

2. Regulatory Affairs Associate

RA teams manage the approval process for medicines and medical devices. Entry-level roles typically involve document management, preparing regulatory submissions, and supporting interactions with health authorities like the FDA or Health Canada. A science degree helps, but so does a detail-oriented mindset and strong writing skills. Some people enter regulatory affairs from a completely non-science background — the writing and analytical skills matter just as much.

3. Quality Assurance Associate

QA is the industry's conscience. Every product that reaches a patient has passed through a quality process. Entry-level QA roles often involve document control, supporting internal audits, and managing SOPs. This is one of the more accessible entry points if you're strong on process and organisation, even without a highly specialised scientific background.

4. Pharmaceutical Sales Representative

You don't need a science degree for pharma sales, but you do need drive, confidence, and a genuine talent for building relationships. Companies care far more about your ability to communicate compellingly and your coachability than your technical knowledge at the entry level — the product training is built into the role. If you have a commercial or customer-facing background, this is worth looking at seriously.

5. Laboratory Technician / Research Associate

If you want to stay close to the bench science, lab roles at pharmaceutical companies, CROs, or contract testing labs are a solid entry point. You'll develop practical technical skills — analytical chemistry, cell culture, sample processing — that transfer across the industry and can eventually pivot towards R&D, quality control, or even regulatory roles.

How to Make Your Non-Pharma Experience Pharma-Relevant

This is where most people undersell themselves. You don't need a pharma job title on your CV to have pharma-relevant skills. You need to translate what you've done into language that hiring managers in this industry recognise.

Some direct translations:

  • Nursing or pharmacy background? You understand patient safety, medication management, adverse event recognition, and clinical documentation — all directly relevant to clinical research and pharmacovigilance.
  • Project management experience in any sector? Pharma runs on timelines, cross-functional teams, and deliverables. Frame it in those terms: budget management, stakeholder communication, milestone tracking.
  • Worked in a regulated environment — food safety, financial services, NHS, government? Emphasise your comfort with strict procedures, audit trails, and compliance documentation.
  • Data, writing, or communications background? Regulatory writing, medical writing, and health economics all need exactly those skills. Be explicit about the type of documents you've produced and for what audience.

On your CV and LinkedIn, frame your experience in terms of outcomes and skills — not just job titles. "Managed compliance documentation for a 30-person team under strict regulatory standards" lands very differently from "Administrative assistant." Same experience, completely different signal.

Certifications That Signal Genuine Interest

You don't need to go back to university. A small number of targeted certifications can make a real difference in how seriously a recruiter takes your application — because they show you've done your homework before anyone paid you to.

  • GCP (Good Clinical Practice) — this is non-negotiable for clinical research roles. Free or low-cost training is widely available through the CITI Program, NIHR Learn, and TransCelerate. Do it before you apply.
  • RAPS Fundamentals — the Regulatory Affairs Professionals Society offers an online Fundamentals of Regulatory Affairs course that's recognised globally. It's a strong starting point if you're targeting RA roles.
  • ICH Guidelines — most candidates skip actually reading these. Don't. The core guidelines (E6 for GCP, Q10 for pharmaceutical quality systems, E2A for pharmacovigilance) are publicly available and reading them puts you ahead of 80% of entry-level applicants.
  • LinkedIn Learning or Coursera pharma courses — less prestigious on their own, but they demonstrate self-direction. Include them if the content is directly relevant to the role you're applying for.

The goal isn't to collect certificates. It's to signal that you're the kind of person who invests in their own development. That signal matters at the entry level when you have little else to differentiate yourself.

LinkedIn and Community — Your Actual Secret Weapon

Here's something the job boards won't tell you: most pharma roles — especially entry-level ones — are not filled purely through online applications. They're filled through conversations. Someone mentions a role to a contact. A recruiter reaches out to a profile they bookmarked. A hiring manager recommends a candidate they met at an event. You need to be inside those conversations.

Practical steps that actually move the needle:

  • Update your LinkedIn headline to make your target clear. "Life Sciences Graduate | Open to CRA / Regulatory Affairs Roles" is infinitely more useful than "Recent Graduate." Recruiters search by keywords — help them find you.
  • Follow pharmaceutical companies, CROs, and industry thought leaders. Engage with their content thoughtfully. You become recognisable before you've sent a single application.
  • Join communities for Black women in pharma — BGIP, Black Women in Science and Engineering (BWISE), and relevant LinkedIn groups. These spaces share unadvertised opportunities, mentors, and honest insight you simply won't get from a company's careers page.
  • Reach out directly — not to ask for a job, but to ask for 20 minutes of someone's time. Most people will say yes to a genuine, specific request for insight. "I'm a biology graduate exploring CRA roles and I'd love to hear about your experience at [Company]" is enough. One warm introduction is worth fifty cold applications.

A Realistic Timeline: What to Expect in the First 6–12 Months

Here's the honest version: breaking into pharma rarely happens in four weeks. And when it takes longer than that, it is not a reflection of your worth or your potential. The industry is competitive, the hiring cycles are slow, and — let's be real — navigating a predominantly white industry as a Black woman adds a layer of friction that most career advice conveniently ignores.

Months 1–2: Build the foundation. Research the roles you're interested in in depth. Complete your GCP certification. Update your CV and LinkedIn. Identify 10–15 companies and individual people to follow and engage with.

Months 3–4: Apply strategically. Target CROs, contract labs, and smaller pharma companies — they tend to have more flexible hiring criteria than large multinationals. Apply to a mix of graduate programmes and specific entry-level roles. Quality over volume, but don't be precious about it — aim for 5–8 well-tailored applications per week.

Months 5–6: Follow up and stay visible. If you haven't heard back after two weeks, one polite follow-up is appropriate. Keep attending virtual events, engaging on LinkedIn, and reaching out for informational conversations. The right opportunity often comes from an unexpected direction — a contact of a contact, a company you'd never heard of, a role that wasn't on your radar.

Months 7–12: Reassess and adapt. If you're not getting callbacks, review your materials honestly — or better yet, ask someone already working in pharma to review them. Consider whether a temporary or contract role could give you the direct experience that accelerates everything. Many people land their first pharma role through a short-term contract that converts to permanent. Don't rule it out.

The journey is rarely linear, and it's not always fair. But it is possible — and you have a community behind you.

Your Break-Into-Pharma Checklist

Before you close this tab, pick three things from this list and do them today:

  • Identify your target entry-level role: CRA, RA Associate, QA Associate, Pharma Sales, or Lab Technician
  • Update your CV to use pharma-relevant language for the experience you already have
  • Update your LinkedIn headline to reflect your target role and industry
  • Enrol in a GCP certification course (CITI Program or NIHR Learn — both free)
  • Research and shortlist 10–15 target companies and CROs
  • Connect with 5 people working in your target area on LinkedIn this week
  • Join Black Girl In Pharma and at least one other industry community
  • Set a 90-day application target and track it somewhere you'll actually see it
