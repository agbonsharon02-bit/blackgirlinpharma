A Day in the Life of a Clinical Research Associate

Early flights, mountains of documentation, and the quiet satisfaction of knowing patients are safe because of your work. Here's what the CRA role actually looks like — no career website version.

6 min read · May 2026
  1. Home
  2. ›
  3. Articles
  4. ›
  5. Day in the Life: CRA

Two Very Different Days

The first thing to understand about being a CRA is that there isn't one kind of day. There are office days and there are site visit days, and they feel completely different. Most CRAs split their week between the two — roughly three office days and two site days, though the balance shifts depending on the study phase, site caseload, and whether you work for a CRO or a sponsor company.

Here's what both actually look like:

Office Day
8:00Triage overnight emails from sites, sponsors, and the clinical team
9:00Write up the monitoring visit report from Friday's site visit
10:30Protocol deviation review call — a site enrolled someone outside the age window
11:30Update CTMS with visit outcomes and flag outstanding action items
12:30Lunch — actually taken, because you're not in a hospital corridor today
1:30Call with a site coordinator to prep for next week's monitoring visit
3:00Review and respond to a site query about informed consent documentation
4:00Read new protocol amendment; flag questions for manager
5:00Log off. No 5:45am alarm tomorrow.
Site Visit Day
5:45Alarm. You're travelling to a hospital site today.
7:30Arrive at site — sign in, set up in the conference room they've given you
8:00Pre-visit meeting with site coordinator: issues since last visit, any deviations to discuss
8:30Source Data Verification — cross-referencing medical records against eCRF entries, subject by subject
12:00Working lunch — usually at the site or a nearby café
1:00Continue SDV; check investigator site file, consent forms, drug accountability log
3:30Close-out meeting: debrief findings, confirm action items, agree timelines
4:30Travel back; mentally draft the visit report on the train
6:30Home. Expense report due by Friday.

Neither day is leisurely. The site day has more energy and variety; the office day requires more sustained concentration and independent judgement. Both demand the same thing: precision.

What CRAs Actually Spend Most of Their Time Doing

Ask someone what a CRA does and they'll say "monitor clinical trials." That's technically correct and completely inadequate. Here's the honest breakdown of where a typical CRA's time actually goes:

  • 35–40% writing and updating reports — monitoring visit reports, follow-up letters, deviation documentation, action item logs
  • 25–30% administrative work — CTMS updates, expense filing, scheduling, email, sponsor reporting
  • 20–25% source data verification — the actual on-site or remote records review work
  • 10–15% cross-functional communication — sponsor calls, investigator training, protocol deviation discussions, team meetings

The administrative load surprises almost everyone entering the role. You will spend considerably more time writing reports about your site visits than you will spend at the sites themselves. That is not a flaw in the system — accurate, audit-ready documentation is a regulatory requirement and a patient safety issue. But it is something to know going in. If you genuinely dislike writing and find detailed admin work draining, this role will exhaust you. If you're someone who can get into the discipline of clear, thorough documentation, you'll find a rhythm.

The Parts Most CRAs Love

The patient connection, even at a remove

CRAs don't treat patients. But they monitor the trials that might one day cure them. Visiting an oncology site and verifying data for a patient enrolled in a first-in-class immunotherapy is a different kind of meaningful. Most CRAs develop a quiet sense of purpose around data accuracy — because they understand, concretely, what inaccurate data means for a patient's safety and for the integrity of the evidence that will guide clinical decisions for years.

The autonomy

Unlike most early-career roles in pharma, a CRA operates with significant self-direction from quite early on. You plan your own site visit schedule, manage your own portfolio of sites, and work largely independently. For people who find too much hand-holding more frustrating than helpful, this independence is one of the job's best features.

The variety and the credential it builds

No two protocols are the same. No two sites are the same. After a few years as a CRA across different therapeutic areas — oncology, neurology, infectious disease, rare conditions — you build a breadth of clinical knowledge that's genuinely hard to get elsewhere. That breadth, combined with the regulatory rigour of the monitoring role, opens significant doors. CRA experience is respected across clinical operations, regulatory affairs, project management, medical writing, and eClinical systems. It's one of the best career-building investments in pharma.

The Parts That Catch People Off Guard

Nobody tells you about the 5:45am alarm when you're interviewing for the role. Nobody tells you about the site coordinator who is excellent at her job and also managing twelve other CRAs simultaneously and doesn't have time for your non-urgent query today. Here's what consistently surprises new CRAs in their first year:

Honest Pros & Cons

The Real Pros

  • High autonomy from early on
  • Direct contribution to patient safety
  • Strong salary and fast progression
  • Variety across sites and therapeutic areas
  • Builds a credential respected across pharma
  • Largely remote-flexible when not travelling

The Real Cons

  • Admin and reporting load is higher than expected
  • 30–60% travel affects work-life balance
  • Can feel isolating without a nearby team
  • Sponsor deadline pressure is relentless
  • Site quality varies wildly — some are a joy, some are not
  • Early-morning flights never feel glamorous by year two

The variability in site quality is worth dwelling on. Some sites are exceptionally well-run: engaged investigators, organised coordinators, clean source documents, zero consent issues. Others are perpetually behind, hard to reach, running too many concurrent trials, and managing staff turnover that means you're re-training someone every few visits. You manage both kinds of site with the same professionalism — but the emotional labour of the latter is real, and it accumulates.

What Travel Really Looks Like

Travel frequency varies significantly by employer and study assignment. CROs tend to carry higher travel loads than sponsor companies. Most entry-level CRAs in Canada can expect:

  • 30–60% travel time over the course of a year — weeks with two overnight trips, weeks with none
  • Mostly domestic: Toronto, Montreal, Vancouver, Ottawa, Calgary, Halifax — wherever major research hospitals and clinics are
  • Trips are typically 1–2 nights, occasionally 3–4 for remote sites
  • International travel for global multi-site trials, more common at Senior CRA level
  • All travel covered: flights or car rental, accommodation, meals, incidentals via expense account

The honest trajectory: in your first year, the travel is often exciting. New cities, independence, the novelty of each site. By year three, Monday morning flights feel less like adventure and more like a tax on your weekends. Many CRAs find their sustainable rhythm and build a life around the travel pattern. Others transition toward more office-based or home-based senior roles as they progress. A few genuinely love the nomadic aspect long-term. Know your baseline tolerance before you commit — it's not something that gets easier to ignore over time.

Career Growth: CRA I Through to Manager

  • 1

    Entry Level · 0–2 Years

    CRA I

    Work under close oversight from a senior CRA or clinical lead. Manage a small portfolio of 3–5 sites. Learn GCP, protocol specifics, monitoring standards, and reporting requirements.

    $55K–$70K CAD
  • 2

    Mid-Level · 2–4 Years

    CRA II

    Independently manage a larger site portfolio across more complex protocols. Begin mentoring CRA Is. Take lead on investigator meetings and protocol deviation escalations.

    $70K–$90K CAD
  • 3

    Senior Level · 4–7 Years

    Senior CRA

    Manage high-priority and complex studies. Contribute to SOP development and site qualification processes. Represent the team at sponsor meetings and investigator training events.

    $90K–$115K CAD
  • 4

    Leadership · 7+ Years

    CRA Manager / Clinical Lead

    Manage a team of CRAs, oversee study-level quality, and interface directly with sponsors on performance and escalations. Often the bridge between field teams and clinical operations leadership.

    $115K–$145K CAD

Beyond CRA Manager, the paths diverge: clinical operations director, project management, regulatory affairs, medical writing, eClinical systems, or independent consulting. The field experience is respected across all of them.

What CRAs Wish They'd Known Before Starting

"The site coordinator is the most important person in a trial, and learning how to genuinely work with them — not just monitor them — is the actual skill of the job. Nobody teaches you that in your GCP training."

"I wish I'd understood that being technically correct isn't always enough. You need enough relational intelligence to get a site to actually fix a deviation, not just acknowledge it."

"Start good documentation habits from day one. Your visit reports at month twelve will be so much stronger than at month one — but the earlier you build the discipline, the faster you get there and the fewer corrections you'll have to make."

"It's okay not to know the answer on the spot at a site. Saying 'I'll confirm that with the team and follow up by end of week' is a professional response. Guessing is not."

"The isolation caught me off guard. When you're travelling alone and working remotely, you have to be proactive about building your professional network. It doesn't happen automatically the way it does in an office."

Introverts, Extroverts, or Both?

Most people assume CRA is an extrovert's job — you're building site relationships, presenting at investigator meetings, and managing people you see only a few times a year. The relational dimension is real and does require genuine warmth and social energy.

But the role rewards introvert strengths just as significantly: deep attention to detail, comfort working alone for extended periods, meticulous independent document review, self-directed time management. The four-hour SDV blocks require focused, quiet concentration that many extroverts find quietly draining.

The CRAs who tend to thrive are what you might call structured relators — people who are genuinely warm and engaged in their site interactions and equally comfortable with long stretches of focused, independent work afterwards. If you have strong people skills and strong documentation discipline, this role will suit you very well. If you have one without the other, you'll find parts of it hard.

Is CRA Right for You? Check These First

Answer these honestly. The more "yes" answers you have, the better the fit:

  • You are genuinely comfortable working independently for long stretches without needing team energy around you
  • You can write clearly and thoroughly — and you don't mind that a significant part of your job involves writing detailed reports
  • You have a high tolerance for travel: early starts, airports, hotel rooms, and overnight stays away from home
  • You can build trust with people you see only a few times a year and maintain those relationships under pressure
  • You care about precision — not just as a professional standard but because you understand what's at stake if documentation fails
  • You're comfortable holding a site accountable when something isn't right, even when the relationship matters and the conversation is uncomfortable
  • You can manage competing priorities without someone telling you what to do next — the role is self-directed and that energises rather than anxiety-inducing for you
  • You're genuinely motivated by the knowledge that clinical trials need monitoring to protect patients, and that this is the work you'd be doing every day

If most of those resonated, CRA is worth pursuing seriously. If several gave you pause, that's not a disqualifier — it's information. Consider whether those gaps are things you can grow into or things that would drain you over a three-to-five year career. Both are valid answers.
